# Origin and Provenance

The origins of *The Wisdom Balance™* lie in a long professional inquiry into the realities of initiative leadership. By around 2011, that inquiry had become sharper as I was exposed more closely to how a layer of management exercised responsibility in complex organizational settings.

The reported problems with initiatives did not always stem from the absence of processes, reporting, or governance. In several cases, the visible structures were present. Meetings were held. Reports were produced. Controls existed. Responsibilities were formally assigned.

Yet the deeper system was often out of balance.

- Some responsibilities were performed rather than owned.
- Some decisions were delayed, softened, or displaced.
- Some governance forums created the appearance of control without producing clarity, accountability, or correction.
- Such behavior was not always penalized. In some environments, it was even rewarded because it preserved appearances, avoided discomfort, or protected institutional narratives.

These observations stayed with me. Over the next few years, I began to look beyond visible delivery pressures and examine the deeper forces that shaped initiative movement. By around 2015, this inquiry had developed into *The Wisdom Balance™*.

As I was formalizing the model, I used it in a few professional situations, especially where the real issue was not the absence of governance, but governance theater: visible controls without sufficient truth, decision, accountability, or corrective action.

> **The model became part of a personal and professional inquiry into the realities of initiative leadership.**

At the time, I found myself increasingly dissatisfied with the mechanical way in which many established project management frameworks described the delivery of initiatives. Processes, tools, techniques, and governance structures were useful, but they did not fully reflect what I had repeatedly observed in practice. Initiatives did not move only through plans, schedules, budgets, controls, and reports. They moved through people, interests, judgment, hesitation, ambition, conflict, values, uncertainty, and the shifting conditions around them.

This dissatisfaction began a quest to represent the reality of initiative leadership in a single visual model.

What followed was a long period of observation and reflection. During this time a moment became decisive.

While looking again at the familiar project management triangle, I realized it did not reflect the reality I experienced. In practice, the triangle was upside down. It did not rest comfortably on a stable base. It balanced precariously on its point. The leader’s task was to keep it upright, steady, and aligned while the forces of the initiative continued to move around it.

That insight became the beginning of *The Wisdom Balance™*.

The following months were spent identifying the deeper forces that influence initiatives beyond the visible pressures of scope, time, cost, and quality. The question was no longer limited to how leaders manage delivery constraints. The deeper question was how leaders maintain equilibrium in a living system shaped by human behavior, decisions, shared values, and controls.

Over time, four balancing forces emerged:

1. **People**
2. **Decisions**
3. **Shared Values**
4. **Controls**

These forces became the core structure of the model. At the center was wisdom: the leadership capability to interpret context, balance competing forces, and determine prudent responses that preserve equilibrium.

The model was later published in **Sun Tzu’s The Art of War in the Battlefield of Project Management**, where it appeared as part of a broader exploration of initiative leadership and the delivery of desired outcomes in complex delivery environments.

This public version presents ***The Wisdom Balance™*** as an independent framework for leadership reflection, initiative diagnosis, and equilibrium in complex delivery environments. It separates the model from the surrounding book narrative, making its structure, concepts, and application method available as a standalone professional framework.

The model remains rooted in project and initiative leadership, but its purpose is broader than project mechanics. It is a way to examine how leaders preserve alignment, stability, and purpose when initiatives are exposed to misalignment, uncertainty, change, chaos, complexity, politics, and human will.
