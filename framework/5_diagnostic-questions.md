# Diagnostic Questions

*The Wisdom Balance* can be used as a leadership diagnostic to examine the condition of an initiative. The questions in this section help leaders move beyond visible pressure and identify the deeper imbalance affecting progress, alignment, and confidence.

These questions are not intended to produce mechanical answers. They are meant to provoke leadership judgment. A question may reveal a governance
gap, a weakness in decision-making, a conflict among stakeholders, a loss of trust, a value compromise, or a misreading of the operating environment.

The value of the diagnostic lies in honest inquiry. Leaders should use these questions during initiation, planning, governance reviews, escalation discussions, recovery efforts, phase transitions, and lessons learned. They may be used individually for reflection or collectively with sponsors, leadership teams, governance bodies, and delivery teams.

> The list of questions for each section below is indicative.
>
> They may not apply to all initiatives, including yours. Your initiative may demand assessment around additional questions. The reader is advised to conduct a diagnosis based on the nature, condition, and realities of their initiatives.

## 1. Reading the Initiative Context

Before examining individual forces, the leader must understand the condition in which the initiative is moving.

- What is the initiative trying to achieve now?

- Has the original intent remained clear, or has it shifted?

- What has changed since the initiative began?

- Which assumptions still hold, and which ones require validation?

- What is visible in formal reports but absent from informal conversations?
  
- What is being discussed informally but absent from formal reports?

- Where is the initiative gaining momentum?

- Where is the initiative losing energy?

- What is being tolerated because it is uncomfortable to confront?

- What truth is the initiative revealing through delay, conflict, rework, or hesitation?

## 2. Identifying Visible Pressure

Visible pressure is the point at which imbalance first becomes noticeable. It may appear through schedule, cost, scope, quality, risk, resources, stakeholder confidence, or value.

- Where is pressure most visible?

- Is the pressure related to scope, time, cost, quality, value, risk, resources, or expectations?
  
- Is the pressure new, recurring, or worsening?

- What are the formal indicators showing?

- What are the informal signals suggesting?

- Is the visible pressure a cause, a symptom, or both?

- What explanation is being repeated most often?

- What explanation is being avoided?

- Which stakeholder is most concerned, and why?

- What would happen if no corrective action were taken?

## 3. People

The People force examines the initiative’s human condition. It considers motivation, trust, self-interest, stakeholder behavior, team capability, collaboration, conflict, and morale.

- Are the right people engaged at the right level?

- Do the people involved understand the initiative’s purpose?

- Are stakeholders aligned in intent, expectation, and behavior?

- Are formal stakeholders and informal influencers both understood?

- Is the team capable of delivering what is expected?

- Is the team motivated, tired, confused, defensive, or divided?

- Are people collaborating because they are committed, or complying because they must?
  
- Are conflicts being surfaced, suppressed, or redirected into politics?

- Is self-interest supporting the initiative or working against it?

- Are people speaking honestly, cautiously, or performatively?

- Is there enough trust for difficult information to travel upward?

- Who benefits from the current confusion, delay, or ambiguity?

- Who is silently carrying the burden of unresolved problems?

- What human reality is the current plan failing to acknowledge?

## 4. Decisions

The Decision force examines how direction is created, clarified, delayed, communicated, and acted upon.

- Are key decisions being made at the right level?

- Are decision rights clear?

- Are decisions timely, or are they delayed by ambiguity, fear, politics, or excessive consensus?
  
- Are decisions based on reliable, relevant, and timely information?

- Are leaders distinguishing between facts, assumptions, opinions, and preferences?
  
- Are previous decisions still valid under current conditions?

- Are decisions being communicated clearly to those who must act on them?
  
- Are decisions creating movement or generating further confusion?

- Are risks, constraints, threats, and opportunities being considered together?
  
- Do the decisions protect the initiative’s purpose or merely reduce leadership’s immediate discomfort?
  
- Which decision has been avoided for too long?

- What decision would wise leadership make if reputation, politics, and fear were set aside?
  
- What consequence of a past decision is now being treated as a new problem?

## 5. Shared Values

The Shared Values force examines the ethical, cultural, and behavioral foundation of the initiative.

- Is the initiative still aligned with the organization’s purpose?

- Are declared values influencing real behavior?

- Are leaders modeling the conduct they expect from others?

- Are behavioral expectations clear and consistently applied?

- Is the team committed to the outcome, or is it merely focused on completing assigned work?
  
- Are stakeholder relationships guided by trust, transparency, and mutual respect?
  
- Are ethical concerns being surfaced and addressed?

- Is the organization rewarding behavior that contradicts its stated values?
  
- Are compromises being made consciously, or quietly normalized?

- Is there a gap between what the organization says and what the initiative tolerates?
  
- Are people proud of how the initiative is being delivered?

- What value is currently being tested?

- What value has become negotiable under pressure?

## 6. Controls

The Control force examines governance, oversight, reporting, risk discipline, change management, escalation, and accountability.

- Are governance structures clear and useful?

- Are roles, responsibilities, and decision rights understood?

- Are controls creating visibility or merely producing documentation?

- Do reports reveal reality or preserve appearances?

- Are risks and issues owned, tracked, and acted upon?

- Are escalation paths working?

- Is change being governed deliberately or absorbed informally?

- Are controls proportionate to the initiative’s complexity and risk?

- Are there too many controls slowing movement?

- Are there too few controls allowing drift?

- Is performance reporting helping leaders decide, or only helping them observe?
  
- Are audits and reviews improving the initiative or creating defensive behavior?
  
- What control is missing?

- What control has become ritual without insight?

## 7. Locating the Imbalance

After examining the four forces, the leader must identify where equilibrium is being lost.

- Which force is currently weakest?

- Which force is dominating the others?

- Which force is being neglected?

- Which force appears healthy on paper but weak in practice?

- Where are two forces in conflict?

- Are controls compensating for weak trust?

- Are people compensating for weak decisions?

- Are decisions being distorted by politics?

- Are values being sacrificed to protect schedule or cost?

- Is governance restraining chaos or concealing it?

- Where is the initiative’s motion concealing the gaps that leadership has not yet plugged?
  
- What pattern has returned despite previous interventions?

- What would the system look like if it were balanced?

## 8. Determining Prudent Response

In this model, wisdom is applied through a prudent leadership response. This means choosing an action that fits the context, restores balance, protects purpose, and avoids creating deeper harm.

- What is the most prudent response under current conditions?

- What should be corrected immediately?

- What should be observed before action is taken?

- What conversation must now happen?

- What decision can no longer be postponed?

- What expectation must be reset?

- What truth must be spoken with care and clarity?

- What must be protected: people, value, trust, ethics, time, quality, or strategic intent?
  
- Where is restraint wiser than intervention?

- Where is intervention wiser than patience?

- Where is escalation necessary?

- Where would escalation create avoidable damage?

- What action restores balance without creating a deeper imbalance?

- What would a prudent, courageous, and ethical leader do now?

## 9. Restoring Equilibrium

Restoration means bringing the initiative back into purposeful movement. It should be seen in behavior, decisions, governance, confidence, and progress.

- Has the initiative regained clarity of purpose?

- Are people more aligned and willing to act?

- Are decisions moving with greater discipline?

- Are shared values influencing conduct under pressure?

- Are controls revealing reality and enabling correction?

- Has the visible pressure reduced, stabilized, or become better understood?
  
- Has the team regained focus?

- Are stakeholders clearer about expectations and trade-offs?

- Has trust improved?

- Has communication become more honest and useful?

- Has the initiative moved from reaction to direction?

- What evidence shows that equilibrium is being restored?

## 10. Continuous Reassessment

When a system is in motion, balance is temporary. Leaders must continue to reassess the initiative as conditions change.

- What new pressure has emerged?

- What has changed in the organization, market, technology, team, or stakeholder environment?
  
- Which assumption now requires fresh validation?

- Which force is beginning to weaken?

- Which control has become outdated?

- Which decision needs to be revisited?

- Which stakeholder has shifted position?

- Which value is likely to be tested next?

- What early signal should leadership not ignore?

- What must be watched with discipline over the next phase?

- When should the next Wisdom Balance review occur?

## Summary Diagnostic View

| Diagnostic Area | Leadership Focus |
|----|----|
| Initiative Context | Understand the environment, assumptions, movement, and hidden signals. |
| Visible Pressure | Identify where stress is appearing in delivery or expectations. |
| People | Examine motivation, trust, capability, conflict, and stakeholder behavior. |
| Decisions | Examine decision quality, timing, authority, information, and consequence. |
| Shared Values | Examine purpose, ethics, culture, conduct, and commitment. |
| Controls | Examine governance, reporting, risk, change, oversight, and accountability. |
| Imbalance | Identify the weak, excessive, neglected, or conflicting force. |
| Wisdom | Determine the prudent leadership response. |
| Equilibrium | Restore purposeful alignment and movement. |
| Reassessment | Continue reading the system as conditions change. |

## Conclusion

The diagnosis should be used with seriousness and candor. 

Used with honest reflection, a diagnostic exercise will help leaders maintain alignment in a moving system. Used poorly, it will become another layer of language over unresolved problems.

> The most useful answer is often the one that makes leadership pause.

*The Wisdom Balance* becomes valuable when it helps leaders see what the system has been trying to show them.
